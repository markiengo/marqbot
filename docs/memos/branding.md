# MarqBot Brand Voice
Version: 3
Audience: Marquette undergraduates, starting with the College of Business

This is the working source of truth for how MarqBot should sound across the frontend.

The short version:

- Be clear before clever.
- Be funny in a dry way, not a loud way.
- Roast the system, not the student.
- Sound student-built, not startup-polished.
- Keep the plan credible even when the line has attitude.

## One-line definition

MarqBot is the student-built planner that reads the degree rules so you do not have to.

## The voice in one paragraph

MarqBot should sound like the upperclassman who already read the bulletin, already got humbled by prereq chains, and would rather save you the trouble. It is calm, concise, useful, a little over CheckMarq, and mildly dramatic in a way that feels earned. It is never corporate, never admin-software stiff, and never a meme account doing bits instead of helping.

MarqBot helps Marquette students figure out what to take next using real degree logic.

It should make students feel:

## Voice rules

### 1. Logic first, punchline second

Every line has a job. The joke is optional. The job is not.

Good:

> This course blocks three others. Handle it early.

Better:

> This course blocks three others. It is doing gatekeeper behavior. Handle it early.

Bad:

> Oopsie bestie. This class is gatekeeping and being toxic.

### 2. Explain the consequence, then add attitude

The best MarqBot lines follow this shape:

1. State the fact.
2. State why it matters.
3. Add one dry twist if there is room.

Example:

> Junior standing required. You are not there yet.

Funnier version:

> Junior standing required. You are not there yet. Senior energy can wait.

### 3. Roast the system, not the student

Allowed targets:

- prereq chains
- bottlenecks
- CheckMarq
- registration chaos
- weird catalog logic
- timeline pressure

Not allowed:

- making the student sound lazy
- making the student sound clueless
- calling the user's plan a delusion
- jokes that imply "you should have known this"

### 4. Campus references are seasoning

Raynor, O'Brien, AMU, Wisconsin Ave, The Brew, Fiserv, and Schroeder work because they make the voice local. See the expanded reference guide in the Marquette campus section below.

They stop working when every sentence tries to prove it goes here.

Rule:

- one campus reference per line max
- use them more in quips than in core UI
- skip them entirely in labels, warnings, and legal copy
- registration-specific terms (PREP, Advising Week, holds) can appear in landing and about copy where they name the real pain

### 5. Dry beats slang — but slang is not banned

MarqBot is funniest when it sounds mildly unimpressed, not terminally online. But it is built by a student and used by students. It should sound like it actually goes here.

The rule is not "never use slang." The rule is: **one term per line max, and the line still works if the slang vanishes.**

#### Green light (natural in academic context, still hot in 2026)

- **lock in** — focus mode, tunnel vision. The single safest slang term for a planner.
- **cooked** — ruined, done for. Perfect for registration stakes.
- **W / L** — win / loss. Simple, universal, great for progress.
- **aura** — vibe, energy, coolness points. "Graduating early? +1000 aura."
- **lil bro** — affectionate condescension. "Lil bro really thought he could skip the prereq."
- **unc** — the older guy being slightly embarrassing. "Don't be the unc still undeclared."
- **ngl** — not gonna lie. Standard emphasis, deeply embedded.
- **fr fr** — for real for real. Same category as ngl.
- **lowkey / highkey** — subtle / very much. Incredible staying power.
- **no shot** — no way, disbelief. "No shot you're fitting Orgo and Physics together."
- **valid** — acceptable, respectable. "Gap semester? Valid."
- **unhinged** — wild, chaotic (often complimentary). "21 credits is unhinged."
- **canon event** — painful but necessary experience. "Failing your first midterm is a canon event."
- **brainrot** — self-aware internet damage. Good for humor.
- **glazing** — overhyping. "Not glazing, but your schedule is actually perfect."
- **ate / ate that** — nailed it, killed it. "Planned that schedule and left no crumbs."
- **crash out** — do something reckless from stress. "Don't crash out and drop everything."
- **chopped** — messed up, ruined, nonsensical. "That registration window got you chopped."
- **dead** — something is hilariously bad/good. "The waitlist has 47 people. I'm dead."

#### Gaming/narrative cluster (maps naturally to degree planning)

- **side quest** — minors, electives, clubs. Secondary paths.
- **main quest / main-questing** — your core degree path. "Stop side-questing. Main-quest your degree."
- **lore** — backstory, transcript history. "Your transcript has some wild lore."
- **plot armor** — magically avoiding bad outcomes. "Got into every class? Plot armor."
- **NPC** — background character, going through the motions.
- **boss fight** — the hard class or sequence that gates everything.
- **final boss** — the last big requirement before graduation.

#### Yellow light (still understood, use in quips only)

- bet, fire, no cap, on god, hits different, mid, based, era, rizz, delulu, it's giving, slay, bussin, real, understood the assignment, rent free

#### Red light (dead or cringe if a brand uses it)

- bestie, periodt, vibe check, "it's the ___ for me," fam, lit, savage, yeet, flex, skibidi, gyatt, sigma, GOAT (in copy), cheugy

#### The stacking rule

Never put two slang terms in the same sentence. One per line. If a line has slang and a campus reference, pick one. The moment it sounds like a meme account, it lost.

### 6. Serious states stay serious

Do not make these playful:

- official disclaimers
- data errors
- blocked submissions
- destructive actions
- missing prerequisites in warning states
- "double-check with your advisor" language

Humor can sit next to a serious message, but it should not dilute it.

## The MarqBot humor model

The funny version of MarqBot is:

- specific
- observational
- lightly cinematic
- slightly over the bureaucracy
- short enough to scan fast

The unfunny version of MarqBot is:

- random
- slang-stacked
- founder-self-owning
- try-hard
- vague
- cute during serious moments

## Humor devices to use

### System personification

- This class controls your timeline.
- That prereq is not bluffing.
- This sequence does not care about your senior-year plans.

### Mild cinematic framing

- origin story
- final stretch
- boss fight
- side quest
- land the plane

### Dry approval

- Clean path.
- Good move.
- Efficient. Respect.
- We love to see it.
- That's a W.
- Ate that.

### Useful understatement

- Not ideal.
- A little aggressive.
- Ambitious.
- That gets messy fast.
- You're cooked.
- Ngl, that's bold.

### Slang-laced observations

Use these in quips, celebrations, empty states. Not in labels or warnings.

- Lil bro really thought he could skip the prereq chain.
- You're lowkey ahead of schedule. Rare behavior.
- No shot you're fitting all of that into one semester.
- This prereq chain is unhinged.
- Your transcript has lore.
- Canon event: realizing you needed that class two semesters ago.
- +500 aura for finishing the business core early.
- Don't crash out and switch majors three weeks before registration.

## Devices to limit

- stacking two slang terms in the same line
- exaggerated chaos
- too many ellipses
- too many rhetorical questions
- stacking two jokes into one sentence
- repeated "rules, not vibes" everywhere
- any red-light slang from the list above

That last line is still good. It is just too important to become wallpaper.

## Humor budget by surface

| Surface | Humor level | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Legal and disclaimer copy | None | Stay plain and direct |
| Errors and validation | Low | Name the issue and the fix |
| Onboarding labels and helper text | Low | Reassuring, not showy |
| Landing page | Medium | One dry hit per block is enough |
| Planner explainer copy | Medium | Sound confident, not chatty |
| Empty states and celebrations | Medium-high | Best place for personality |
| Quips | High | Still short, still grounded |
| About page | High | Most personality, but keep trust intact |

## Surface guidance

### Landing

Lead with the outcome. Then add one line that acknowledges registration pain.

Good pattern:

- headline says what the tool does
- subhead says why it matters
- one dry jab at the old way

### Onboarding

This is the clearest writing in the product and should stay that way.

- short labels
- plain helper text
- one mild joke max per step
- no forced campus references

### Planner

This is where trust is won or lost.

- headers should sound confident
- warnings should sound calm
- explainers can carry some personality
- action buttons should stay literal

### Saved plans

Do not undercut the feature with self-owning humor.

Saved plans are practical. They are not a bit.

### About

This page gets the widest humor budget, but it still has to protect confidence in the product.

Better:

- joke about the problem being ridiculous
- joke about how long the process took
- joke about campus infrastructure

Worse:

- joke that the product is held together with luck
- joke that quality is probably bad
- joke that the user's bug report is expected because the app is shaky

## Lessons from `quips.csv`

The quips work because they do a few things very well:

- They use concrete nouns instead of abstract filler.
- They make the system or situation the butt of the joke.
- They create a visual in one line.
- They stay short.
- They keep the student competent.

The quips also reveal a trap:

- "energy," "arc," and campus nouns can get repetitive if they leave the quip layer and spread everywhere.

Use the quip voice as inspiration, not as a copy-paste machine.

## Gen Z audience psychology

This section is not about chasing trends. It is about understanding why the existing voice rules work and where to sharpen them.

### Why "student-built" is the strongest trust signal

91% of Gen Z consumers value authenticity. 67% prefer transparent brands over polished marketing. They perceive niche and challenger brands as more trustworthy because those brands "keep their edges intact."

For MarqBot this means: the fact that it was built by a student, with real rough edges, reads as more credible than a slick edtech product ever could. Do not sand it down. Do not add corporate polish. The lack of polish is the trust signal.

### How Gen Z processes humor

Gen Z humor is layered, dry, and self-aware. The key traits:

- **Dry delivery over loud delivery.** Absurd statements delivered with a straight face. The joke does not explain itself.
- **Anti-corporate instinct.** Corporations trying to sound like teenagers are "at the bottom of the food chain." This is why MarqBot should never sound like a brand account doing bits.
- **Self-deprecating about shared struggle, not about the product.** "Registration ate my entire Sunday" works. "Our app is held together with duct tape" does not.
- **Context-dependent.** Inside the community, the meaning is instant. Outsiders miss the joke. This is a feature, not a bug. Marquette-specific references reward insiders.

The existing voice rules already nail this. The main risk is drifting toward try-hard territory. If a line feels like it is performing for an audience instead of talking to a friend, cut it.

### The cringe detector

Gen Z has a finely tuned cringe detector. What triggers it:

- Slang that is 2-3 weeks past peak usage
- Forced humor that tries to invent memes instead of observing real ones
- Overly polished, stock-image aesthetics
- Corporate accounts imitating teenager energy
- Any line that feels calculated rather than natural

What passes the detector:

- Content that is "unapologetically weird" but consistent
- Lo-fi, raw, unpolished-on-purpose tone
- The self-aware wink: it knows it is being a little dramatic and does not pretend otherwise
- Real observations about shared pain, stated plainly

### Speed and attention

Gen Z attention is not short. It is selective. They decide within seconds if something is relevant, then can stay engaged for minutes.

For MarqBot: every screen must answer "why should I care?" immediately. The onboarding must deliver value in seconds, not minutes. The landing page must hook in the first viewport. If the first thing a student sees is filler, they bounce.

### Empowerment over prescription

Gen Z wants self-directed experiences. They want to feel in control of their own path.

Frame MarqBot as: "here is what is true about your degree, and here are your best options" — not "here is what you should do." The student decides. MarqBot just reads the rules faster than they can.

## Marquette campus and registration context

This section documents the real pain points and cultural references that make MarqBot's voice land with Marquette students specifically.

### CheckMarq and registration friction

CheckMarq is the student information system for registration, grades, billing, and degree audits. The friction points students actually experience:

- **PREP module requirement.** Before every advising appointment, students must complete a PREP (Pre-Registration Educational Planning) module. If they do not, an academic hold blocks registration.
- **Advising Week.** Mid-semester, students get a 20-minute advising appointment. That is the window to plan the entire next semester.
- **Academic holds.** If you skip the PREP form or miss your advisor, a hold goes on your account. No registration until it is cleared.
- **Graduation checklist juggling.** Students must share their PREP results plus a graduation checklist with their advisor during the appointment.
- **The "What-if" tool.** CheckMarq has a scenario tool for exploring major/minor changes, but it is not intuitive and does not rank anything.
- **Degree progress reports.** Satisfied requirements collapse; unsatisfied ones expand. The logic is correct but the presentation buries the signal.

These are the real things students are frustrated with. When MarqBot references "registration chaos" or "the old way," this is what it means. The language of "holds," "PREP," and "Advising Week" resonates immediately.

### College of Business advising

Business students use Advising Central (CoBAAdvising@marquette.edu). Same PREP and Advising Week requirements as the broader university. The 20-minute window is especially tight for business students juggling majors, tracks, and minors.

### Marquette cultural context

- **33% of incoming first-years are students of color** (most diverse class in university history). The voice should feel inclusive without being performative.
- **Jesuit values are real, not decorative.** *Cura personalis* (care for the whole person) and community service are genuinely embedded in student culture. "Built to help fellow Golden Eagles" aligns with this. Do not be preachy about it.
- **Basketball is the dominant sports culture.** "We Are Marquette" is the signature chant. Coach Shaka Smart is a recognized figure. Big East Conference. 1977 national championship. Games at Fiserv Forum are major social events.
- **22% are first-generation college students.** Degree planning confusion is not laziness — the system is genuinely opaque for students without family experience navigating it. Never make the student sound clueless.

## Final test for every line

Before shipping a line, ask:

1. Would a busy student understand this on one read?
2. If the joke vanished, would the line still work?
3. Is the joke pointed at the system instead of the student?
4. Is this the right surface for humor?
5. Does this sound like MarqBot, not Twitter and not admin software?

If yes, keep it.
If not, rewrite it.
