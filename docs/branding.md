# MarqBot — Final Brand Positioning & Voice Guidelines
Version: Definitive  
Audience: Marquette Undergraduates (starting with College of Business)

This is the single source of truth for how MarqBot should position itself and communicate across product UI, onboarding, landing pages, and future features.

The goal:  
Be unmistakably student-built.  
Be genuinely funny.  
Be academically reliable.  
Never be cringe.  
Never be corporate.

---

# 1. Core Brand Positioning

## One-Line Summary

MarqBot is your academically responsible but slightly chaotic degree-planning bestie — powered by rules, not vibes.

---

## Core Position Statement

MarqBot helps Marquette students figure out exactly what to take next using deterministic course logic, delivered in a voice that feels like a witty upperclassman who already learned the hard way.

It reduces confusion.  
It prevents prereq disasters.  
It does not guess.

---

## What MarqBot Is

- A deterministic degree planning engine
- A rules-based course recommendation system
- A requirement progress tracker
- A semester mapping tool (up to 8 semesters)
- Built by a Marquette student

---

## What MarqBot Is NOT

- Not CheckMarq
- Not DegreeWorks
- Not your official advisor
- Not an AI chatbot guessing your future
- Not motivational content

---

## Emotional Outcome

After using MarqBot, a student should feel:

- “Okay wait… that makes sense.”
- “I didn’t realize that class blocks three others.”
- “Fine. I’ll take the prereq first.”
- “I’m not spiraling anymore.”

---

# 2. Target Cultural Fit

Marquette students:

- Value community but don’t want corporate tone
- Are busy and academically pressured
- Appreciate humor when it’s real and earned
- Can detect forced slang instantly
- Want clarity fast

MarqBot must sound like:

- A peer who understands prereq pain
- Someone who has read the requirement sheet
- Someone who will call out a bad idea
- Someone who still respects you

---

# 3. Voice Principles

## 1. Clear First, Funny Second

Every line must:
1. Communicate the logic.
2. Then optionally add personality.

If humor reduces clarity, remove it.

---

## 2. Roast the System, Not the Student

Allowed:
- Light sarcasm about prereqs
- Jokes about gatekeeping courses
- Dramatic phrasing about long sequences

Not allowed:
- Mocking the user
- Implying incompetence
- Making them feel dumb

Example:

Good:
> This course is a gatekeeper. It blocks three others. Let’s handle it early.

Also good:
> This course gatekeeps three others. It enjoys chaos. Take it sooner.

Bad:
> You should’ve checked this earlier.

---

## 3. Deterministic Confidence

MarqBot must always communicate:

- Same inputs = same outputs
- No randomness
- No hallucinations
- No vibes

Example:
> Ranked by actual degree logic. Not guesswork.

---

## 4. Slightly Dramatic, Strategically

MarqBot can exaggerate mildly for humor:

- “Four-course saga.”
- “Bold choice.”
- “Ambitious.”
- “We’re not doing that.”

But never over-the-top meme chaos.

# 4. Tone Matrix by Context

| Context | Tone | Humor Level |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| Onboarding | Friendly + curious | Light |
| Recommendations | Direct + confident | Medium |
| Prereq blocker | Calm + witty | Medium |
| Serious academic warning | Calm + factual | Low |
| Near graduation | Encouraging + grounded | Low |
| Help modal | Clear + structured | Light |
| Legal disclaimer | Strict + zero jokes | None |

# 5. Signature Language Bank

Use these types of phrases consistently.

## Approval / Positive States

- We love to see it.
- No blockers. You’re good.
- Clean path.
- Solid move.
- Efficient. Respect.
- Senior energy.

---

## Blockers

- Oopsie. That class is locked.
- Not happening (yet).
- You need [COURSE] first.
- Prereq missing. Let’s fix that.
- Bold choice. Unfortunately, no.
- Ambitious. Not eligible.

---

## Gatekeeping Courses

- This course blocks three others.
- Gatekeeper energy.
- Handle this early.
- Don’t let this bottleneck you.
- This class controls your timeline.

---

## Long Sequences

- Long chain. Start early.

- Future-you will thank you.

---

## Useless Electives (for now)

- This doesn’t move your degree forward yet.
- Fun, but not strategic right now.
- Respectfully… later.

---

## Summer

- Summer semester? Ambitious.
- Summer counts. Plan wisely.
- Grind responsibly.

# 6. Microcopy Standards

## Onboarding

- What’s your major?
- What classes have you finished?
- Let’s build your plan.
- Drop your completed courses. Don’t lie to me.

---

## Recommendation Screen

Header:
> Here’s what to take next.

Subtext:
> Ranked by actual degree logic.

When eligible:
> You can take this.

When missing prereq:
> Oopsie. That class is locked. You need [COURSE].

When standing issue:
> Requires junior standing. You’re not there yet.

When efficient:
> Two requirements. One class. We love efficiency.

---

## Degree Progress

- You’re 82% done.
- This requirement bucket is still hungry.
- Almost there. Don’t fumble now.
- Requirement left: [X].

Avoid:
- “Requirement group incomplete.”
- Institutional phrasing.

---

## Help Modal: How It Works

Structure must be:

1. Can you actually take it?
   If prereqs or offering don’t work, it’s out.

2. What does it count toward?
   Core + major before electives.

3. Is it blocking you?
   Gatekeeper courses move up.

4. How long is the chain?
   Long sequences start earlier.

5. Does it knock out multiple requirements?
   Two-for-one courses rank higher.

Close with:
> I assume you pass everything. Keep your courses updated so I don’t accidentally plan your downfall.

# 8. Writing Constraints

Always:

- Use short sentences.
- Prefer active voice.
- Use “you.”
- Keep paragraphs tight.
- Prioritize scannability.

Never:

- Use corporate buzzwords.
- Use excessive slang.
- Overuse “bestie.”
- Use emojis.
- Sound like a startup pitch deck.

# 11. Research Notes: Audience + Decision Rationale

This section records the reasoning behind the current homepage and onboarding language choices.

The goal is not to sound more startup. The goal is to:

- make the value obvious fast
- reduce first-use friction
- get students to the useful output quickly
- keep personality without slowing comprehension

---

## Audience Insights

The current target audience behaves like this:

- They are stressed, busy, and trying to avoid a bad registration decision.
- They scan first and read second.
- They do not want a lecture before they get value.
- They trust plain language more than polished jargon.
- They respond well to dry humor, but only after the point is clear.
- They care about "what do I take next?" more than "how sophisticated is the engine?"

Implication:

- Lead with the outcome.
- Use student words like "classes," "major," and "plan."
- Push proof right after the promise.
- Keep the witty line as a garnish, not the main course.

---

## What The Research Suggested

Across YC messaging advice and Demand Curve / TechCrunch guidance, the recurring pattern was:

1. Say what the product does in plain English.
2. Make the benefit obvious immediately.
3. Reduce the number of decisions before the user sees value.
4. Use specific CTAs instead of vague ones.
5. Treat onboarding as a fast path to the "aha" moment, not a mini-tour.

In plain terms for MarqBot:

- "Pick your major. Add your classes. Get your plan." beats clever copy that needs decoding.
- "Show My Plan" beats a vague CTA when the final action matters.
- Short setup steps feel safer than dense explanations.
- Students should feel like they are already in the tool, not being sold to inside onboarding.

---

## Wording Decisions

These wording choices are intentional:

- Use "classes" more often than "courses" on first-touch screens.
- Use "plan" instead of internal system language.
- Use "major" before "track" and "concentration" because that matches how students think.
- Put explanation after the headline, not inside it.
- Keep humor to one light hit per block at most.

Why:

- Average students process concrete school words faster.
- The job-to-be-done is practical, not aspirational.
- Overly smart-sounding language makes the product feel less trustworthy, not more.
- Witty lines work better when they confirm the point instead of replacing it.

Examples of the shift:

- Better: "Add what you've already finished."
- Worse: "Input your academic history."

- Better: "Tell MarqBot what kind of plan you want."
- Worse: "Configure recommendation preferences."

- Better: "That program needs a primary major too."
- Worse: "This major must be paired with a primary major to proceed."

---

## Onboarding Design Decisions

The current onboarding structure is designed around speed and stability:

- Keep each step visually focused on entry, not explanation.
- Use compact side guidance on desktop instead of a large header stack.
- Let internal UI pieces scroll before the full page scrolls.
- Keep the step count visible so users know the finish line.
- Use color emphasis only on key words that matter to decision-making.
- Use motion as signposting, not decoration-only theater.

Specific rationale:

- Desktop side rail:
  Keeps context visible without taking vertical space away from the form.

- Compact step headers:
  Reinforces the job of the screen without creating a second homepage above the form.

- Capped chip areas in course entry:
  Users can add many classes without blowing up the entire page height.

- Internal dropdown limits:
  Search results stay usable on shorter laptop screens.

- Gold / blue emphasis on a few words:
  Helps scanning and tells the eye where the important decision is.

- Short transitions and floating accents:
  Make the flow feel modern and alive, but do not slow input.

---

## Messaging Guardrails From This Research

For future homepage, onboarding, and planner copy:

- Clarity first. Always.
- Outcome before mechanism.
- Keep proof concrete, not inflated.
- One joke max per block.
- If a line sounds clever but slows understanding, cut it.
- If a label sounds like admin software, rewrite it.

Default copy order:

1. What this screen does
2. What the student should enter or decide
3. Why it matters
4. Reassurance that it is quick or editable
